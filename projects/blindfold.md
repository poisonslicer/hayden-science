# Blindfold

*A short story — placeholder draft.*

He learned the game the way other kids learned their multiplication tables: out loud, in the dark, on the long drives where there was nothing to look at anyway.

"e4," his father would say.

"e5," he'd answer, and the board would assemble itself behind his eyes — not as a picture exactly, more as a *pressure*, sixty-four little weights arranged in his head, each one leaning on the others.

For thirty years he kept the board in there. Through college, through the lab, through the slow good years and the fast bad one. He never owned a real set. He didn't need one. The board was always with him, and so, in a way, was the man who had taught it to him.

> The hardest move in blindfold chess is not the one you can't see.
> It's the one you can — and play anyway.
